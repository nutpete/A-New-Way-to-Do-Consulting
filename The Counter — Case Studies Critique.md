*A pre-launch review of the case studies on v6, framed against the POV v2 narrative. For Drew to action directly.*

## Where we are

You have three case studies on the site:

1. **Consumer Goods / Materials R&D** → "Absorb" — senior materials scientist, 10 years of data
2. **Financial Services / Dispute Adjudication** → "CaseBuilder" — senior dispute analyst, ~20 cases/day
3. **Life Sciences / Concept Development** → "ConceptLab" — pharma brand lead, concept strategy

They share a consistent structural template: industry tab → hero hook + two metrics → 3-4 tool screenshots → Before/After block → four numbered steps (01–04) → "The Shift" closing. Each has its own designed UI mockups (good — they don't look the same), but the scaffolding around them is identical across all three.

**Your instinct is right: they're 50–60% there.** CaseBuilder and ConceptLab are close. Materials R&D is meaningfully behind. More importantly, there's a structural problem affecting all three that's easy to miss if you only look at one at a time.

The single most important thing I noticed: **the metrics you're using are efficiency metrics, not fluency metrics.** That's a big deal given what the POV just argued.

---

## What the case studies need to do

The POV makes the case studies' job much clearer than it was before. The POV argues, among other things:

- Efficiency ≠ transformation
- Expertise is not what AI replaces, but what it requires
- Fluency is learned through lived experience, not workshops
- The real unlock is discovering problems you'd stopped trying to solve

**If those are the arguments, then every case study is in service of proving one specific claim:** this person's expertise became *more* valuable — not less — because they could now reach parts of their own work they'd given up on.

A case study that reads "she used to spend 6 hours on a report, now she spends 1" is literacy/efficiency language. That's the exact reassurance the POV says is a trap.

A case study that reads "she had ten years of data and had time to test 5 hypotheses per cycle. Now she tests 50" is fluency/transformation language. That's the reframe you need.

**Every case study should answer three questions in the reader's head:**

1. What did this person know that only they could know? *(Expertise)*
2. What were they unable to do because of that gap between their knowledge and their tools? *(Abandoned problem)*
3. What became possible when the gap closed? *(Unlock — not just speed, but range)*

A reader should finish each one thinking not "that looks fast" but "wait — someone at our company is in exactly this situation."

## The four cross-cutting issues

These apply to all three case studies. Fixing them first will do more than polishing any one case.

### 1. The metrics are telling the wrong story

Every case study uses the same two-metric format at the top. All three currently frame efficiency:

| Case | Metric 1 | Metric 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Materials R&D | "60% Time on analysis" | "Days Build time" |
| Concept Development | "4 → 8 Concepts / cycle" | "Days Build time" |
| Dispute Adjudication | "20+ Cases / day" | "Days Build time" |

**Specific problems:**

- **"60% Time on analysis"** is a standalone number with no reference point. 60% of what? Reduced by 60%? And the After reads "Inverted," which forces the reader to do arithmetic to understand what happened. Cute but confusing.

- **"Days Build time"** — "Days" isn't a number, it's a word dressed up as a number. It appears on *all three* case studies in the exact same format. It's a template tic. Either give a real number ("2 days," "3 days") or cut it entirely and put build speed in the body copy.

- **"4 → 8 Concepts / cycle"** is the cleanest of the three — it's a transformation metric. But it's also the least dramatic.

- **"20+ Cases / day"** is a volume, not a change. What changed? If she was already doing 20+, the metric is meaningless.

**The deeper issue:** these metrics anchor the reader in "how much faster" rather than "how much bigger." That's the trap the POV warns against. What did this person do that they *couldn't* do before, at any speed?

**Recommendation — swap to transformation metrics:**

- **Materials R&D**: *Hypotheses tested per cycle: 5 → 50* (unlock of range). Or *Years of data in active use: 1 → 10* (unlock of history). Or even *Questions she could ask the data: limited → unlimited* if you want to be bolder.
- **Concept Development**: Keep "4 → 8" if it's true, but consider stronger framing like *Strategic foundation: outsourced → owned* (this is already the line in the "Before/After" narrative — make it the top metric).
- **Dispute Adjudication**: *Time spent on judgment vs. assembly* or *Cases her expertise actually touches: some → all.*

Pick metrics that say "the scope of what I do changed," not "the speed of what I do improved." The POV has already drawn a line in the sand between those two. The case studies need to land on the right side of it.

### 2. The structure is too templated

Each case study has the exact same skeleton. Different UI in the middle, but the same frame around it. Three case studies in a row with the same beats starts to read as "here's another one" by the second. The reader needs to feel they're seeing fundamentally different *kinds* of transformation, not the same transformation in different industries.

**Recommendation — let each case study's structure reflect what's actually being transformed:**

- **Materials R&D** is about *compression and exploration*. The case should emphasize what she can now *ask* of her data that she couldn't before. Structure: her question → the data waking up → the new questions it opens.
- **Concept Development** is about *role change*. The strategic work used to belong to the agency; now it belongs to her. Structure: the handoff that no longer happens → the strategic foundation she now owns → what the agency becomes instead.
- **Dispute Adjudication** is about *cognitive load redistribution*. She was always fast at the judgment; assembly was the tax. Structure: the tax that's gone → the judgment that remains sovereign (the tool deliberately doesn't propose a verdict — this is the most important beat in all three cases and it's buried).

You could literally use different section headers for each case, in different orders. That visual variation will do more to differentiate them than any copy polish.

### 3. The human disappears

In all three cases, we name the role ("senior materials scientist," "senior dispute analyst," "pharma brand lead"), but we never hear her voice. We never see her reaction. The POV is specifically about the *human experience* of fluency arriving — "discomfort into belief" — and the case studies are third-person observations.

**Compare to what the POV does:** "If the thing I am known for can be done by a machine, what exactly am I for?" That's a voice. That's a real question a real person asks.

**Recommendation — add one pulled-out moment per case where the person speaks or the narrator channels their voice.**

Not faked quotes (that would feel dishonest for hypothetical cases). But you can do something like:

> *"I hadn't looked at the 2017 data in years. I didn't have time. Now I'm running it through a hypothesis I've been sitting on since then."*
>
> — rough paraphrase of what the scientist told us in the reveal

Or even just a narrator line that gives her interior monologue a beat of attention:

> **The first thing she noticed wasn't the speed.** It was that the questions were starting to come from her, not from the backlog of things she'd scheduled to get to.

One line per case. Humanizes the tool without overclaiming.

### 4. The reader's expertise is being assumed

Drew specifically called this out — "handheld, not relying on expertise." Here are places the case studies assume too much:

- **Materials R&D:** "r=+0.412," "Surfactant Level," "weighted composite rankings," "anomaly flagging" — a CEO/CDAO reading this has no entry point.
- **Dispute Adjudication:** "EMV chip-read + PIN first attempt," "AVS Result," "DSP-[BLINDED-01]" — real analyst language, but impenetrable to a VP of Digital.
- **Concept Development:** "SELECT-BEYOND," "ACR20 separation," "MLR in parallel" — pharma insider speak.

The tension: you want the screens to look real, and real tools have jargon. But the *surrounding narrative* can translate without softening the screens.

**Recommendation — use a consistent "translation voice" in the narrative that tells the non-specialist reader what they're looking at.**

Like a good museum caption. Don't remove the jargon from the UI — the jargon IS the realness. But the narrative copy around it should do the work of explaining.

Example — Materials R&D:

> Correlations live now — particle size vs. absorption speed, surfactant level vs. rewet. The tool surfaces which material properties actually predict performance, so she stops manually running comparisons one variable at a time.

That sentence uses the jargon, but the phrase "stops manually running comparisons one variable at a time" tells the reader what was tedious. The jargon becomes texture; the narrative carries meaning.

Right now, the narrative sometimes does this and sometimes doesn't.

## Case-by-case

### Materials R&D (Absorb) — the weakest, needs the most work

**What's working:**

- Opening hook is fine: "She was only using a fraction of it."
- "The Shift" closing line is excellent: *"The tool didn't add anything to the process. It removed everything that was between the expertise and the science."* This is the POV in miniature — save it, build toward it.

**What's not working:**

- **The metrics are the most confusing of the three.** "60% / Inverted" fails the handheld test.
- **The tool name "Absorb" isn't introduced.** It's a cute name for a diaper/absorption R&D context, but a first-time reader doesn't know why. One half-sentence of anchor would fix it.
- **The screens don't tell a story.** They show "580 records loaded / correlations / what-if lab / report PDF" — this is the *architecture* of the tool, not the *arc of her week*. Compare to CaseBuilder, where each screen advances a narrative (DSP case → contradictions → strengths/weaknesses → decision scaffold). Absorb shows features. CaseBuilder shows a thinking process.
- **"Ten years of data, only using a fraction" is abstract.** How much is a fraction? Why couldn't she use it? The POV's strongest lever is the "abandoned problem" — what had she given up on? The narrative dances around this but never lands it.
- **The 01-04 steps are all phrased as capability statements** ("See the patterns / Find the drivers / Explore what's next / Generate the report") rather than transformation statements. The reader learns what the tool *does*, not what *she* now gets to do.
- **No human reaction anywhere.**

**Suggested rewrites:**

*Opening:*
> Sarah has been studying polymer absorption for ten years. Her test cycles produce hundreds of results; every one of them lives in a spreadsheet; most of them sit there unread.
>
> Not because she doesn't care. Because by the time each cycle closes, there are twenty-two new combinations to document, and she's writing up results instead of reading them. She has an instinct that surfactant level interacts with particle size in a non-linear way, but she's never had time to prove it.

*Tool intro:*
> She called it Absorb — partly the domain, partly the joke that her data had been absorbing her time instead of the other way around.

*Reframe the four steps as transformation beats, not feature tours:*

- **01 — Ten years, live.** The data she'd written but not read is now speaking back. Every historical cycle is queryable in context.
- **02 — The hypothesis she'd held onto.** Particle size vs. absorption speed, surfactant vs. rewet. She gets her answer in seconds. The non-linear interaction she suspected is real.
- **03 — The next twenty questions she didn't know she had.** With each answer, the tool generates follow-up correlations. She's no longer asking what she had time for — she's asking what she's curious about.
- **04 — The report writes itself.** The documentation that used to consume her Thursday becomes a byproduct, not a task.

*Close:*
> The tool didn't make her faster at her existing work. It let her get back to the work she'd stopped trying to do.

**Rough rewrite of the metrics:**

- Metric 1: **"Hypotheses tested per cycle: ~5 → 50+"** (transformation language, real number if you want, or "handful → limitless" if you want to stay directional)
- Metric 2: **Cut "Days Build time" from the hero block and mention build speed in body copy.** Or replace with a more interesting statistic: "Years of data reactivated: 10."

### Concept Development (ConceptLab) — strongest of the three, small polish

**What's working:**

- **The hook is the best of the three.** "A pharma brand lead had the strategy in her head. It took three weeks and an agency to get it on paper." That sentence earns the whole rest of the case study.
- **The tool's narrative arc is clear.** Brief → foundations → traceability → patient/HCP parallel. Each screen advances the argument.
- **"Traceable to Source"** is a brilliant beat — it shows the tool supporting her expertise (every concept is tied to the insight that produced it) rather than replacing it. Lean into this; it's your best example of "expertise is what AI requires."
- **"Strategic foundation work outsourced → owned"** is the cleanest line in any of the three cases. This is fluency-language, exactly.

**What needs polish:**

- **"4 → 8 Concepts / cycle"** undersells the case. The real unlock isn't doubling concept volume — it's that the strategic work moved from the agency's desk back to hers. Use that as the metric. "*Strategic work: outsourced → owned*" in a big callout would carry far more weight than "4 → 8."
- **The pharma jargon is dense.** SELECT-BEYOND, ACR20, JAK inhibitor, bio-IR. Either add translation in the narrative or assume your reader is in pharma. (If the Life Sciences tab is only meant to resonate with Life Sciences buyers, maybe fine. If it's also meant to be accessible to a CDAO in financial services reading the site, it's too dense.)
- **No human moment.** Even a line like *"When the first four concepts appeared, she realized something: the agency had been doing this work, but not this way. The strategy had never been this legible."* would land.
- **The 01-04 section titles are more varied than Absorb's** ("The Brief / Concept Foundations / Traceable to Source / Patient + HCP in Parallel") — good. These read as narrative beats, not feature names. Keep this approach and apply it to Materials R&D.

### Dispute Adjudication (CaseBuilder) — very strong, small polish

**What's working:**

- **The best single line in any case study is here:** *"The tool assembles everything needed for the determination and puts the blank determination box next to it. It does not propose a verdict, on purpose. The judgment is hers — the prep is what's been taken off her plate."* This is the POV's thesis expressed as a UI decision. It's the most important sentence on the whole site. **Feature it more prominently** — probably as a pulled quote.
- **The "VERIFIED vs. ASSERTED" distinction** is specific, visual, and uniquely domain-credible. It shows you understand how dispute analysts actually think.
- **The closing line is excellent:** *"The tool didn't make her faster at adjudicating. She was always fast. It stopped making her rebuild the case file twenty times a day."* That's fluency-framing.

**What needs polish:**

- **"20+ Cases / day"** as a metric is the weakest of any of the three. It tells the reader nothing — was she doing 20 before too? The transformation isn't in the number. Replace with something like *"Time spent on judgment vs. assembly: ~10% → ~80%"* (directional, but captures what actually changed).
- **The "Decision Scaffold" screen is your best example of the tool-refusing-to-replace-expertise.** Make sure the caption on that screen lands hard — something like: *"The tool stops here on purpose. The verdict is hers."* That one screen, with that one caption, is worth more than four feature explanations.
- **The case reference "DSP-[BLINDED-01]"** is smart (signals real-case-ness while protecting the hypothetical), but the brackets feel a little over-redacted and pull you out of the fiction. Consider just "DSP-0417" or similar — looks more like a real case ID.
- **No human moment** — same issue as the other two. Even a line where you name the thing she used to dread ("Mondays, when the queue was three deep before she sat down") would ground the case in lived reality.

## Writing voice guidelines

Use these to sharpen the copy across all three cases:

### Handheld specificity

- **Always translate jargon in the narrative, never in the UI.** "Anomaly flagging" in a screen is fine. "Anomaly flagging" in a caption needs to become "the tool catches results that don't match the historical pattern."
- **Use domain words, but explain them on first use through narrative context, not definitions.** "SELECT-BEYOND (the trial that tested Novara in patients whose biologics had stopped working)" is better than a pop-up definition.

### Show the transformation, not the feature

- Replace "The tool does X" with "She used to do Y. Now she does Z."
- The subject of your sentences should usually be the human, not the tool.
- Before: *"The tool surfaces which material properties most predict each performance outcome."*
- After: *"Instead of running comparisons one variable at a time, she can see every predictive relationship at once."*

### Lean into the refused-move

Every case has a moment where the tool could have done more but doesn't. CaseBuilder's decision scaffold. ConceptLab's traceability back to source. Absorb's hypothesis explorer that needs her to dial in the parameters. **These are your most important moments.** They prove the "expertise is what AI requires" claim.

Name them clearly. Pull them out visually. Don't bury them in paragraph four.

### One human moment per case

A single line per case where we hear the person's voice or feel their reaction. Not dramatic. Not long. Just enough to remind the reader that someone is at the other end of this tool, and she is not a robot being optimized.

### Cut "Days Build time" from every hero block

It's a template artifact. It says "we built this fast" three times in a row and the reader notices. Put it in the narrative copy once, not in the metrics area.

## Design differentiation guidance

Right now the three case studies have **different UI mockups but identical scaffolding**. You want the opposite — the scaffolding should also vary so each case feels like a different *kind* of story.

Practical ways to vary:

### Vary the number and order of beats

- **Materials R&D** could be three beats instead of four ("The data wakes up / The hypothesis comes back / The report disappears").
- **Concept Development** could lead with the *before* state more dramatically — show the three weeks of waiting before showing the tool at all.
- **Dispute Adjudication** could foreground the "what the tool doesn't do" beat — put it first or second instead of last.

### Vary the visual rhythm

- One case could be screenshot-heavy, one could be text-heavy with a single hero screen, one could alternate.
- One case could use a timeline/before-after as its dominant visual rather than UI screens.
- One case could open with a quote treatment instead of a metric treatment.

### Vary the framing verb

- Materials R&D is a *reactivation* story.
- Concept Development is a *reclamation* story.
- Dispute Adjudication is a *release* story.

If you can find one framing word per case and theme the visual treatment around it, they'll feel like three distinct stories instead of three rounds of the same template.

### Vary the scale of the tool shown

- Absorb currently shows a full-featured platform with correlations, scenarios, and reporting.
- ConceptLab shows a strategic tool with multi-layered detail.
- CaseBuilder shows a focused utility for a single workflow.

Right now all three look roughly "mid-complexity." Consider making one deliberately *simpler*-looking — a single screen, a single button — to signal "this is how little it can take." And one more elaborate — to signal "this is how far it can go." The variation in visual density helps them feel like different scales of solution, which they genuinely are.

## What's missing entirely

A few things the case studies don't currently do that they probably should:

### The counter log

The POV and your engagement model both mention that every conversation — not just every build — gets documented. That's an interesting part of the model and it's invisible on the site. A small sidebar or accompanying artifact ("This case study started as one of seventeen conversations we had over three days. Here's what the others surfaced.") would reinforce that the engagement produces more than the tools you happen to feature.

### The 30-day later

Each case study ends at the reveal. What happened after? Did she use it? What did she change? What did she build next? A one-line postscript — "Two weeks later, she'd run seventeen more hypotheses and submitted three for the next cycle review" — does more to prove the fluency claim than any upfront metric. Even if you're writing these as hypothetical scenarios, a written postscript is the fluency payoff.

### The "this is what you would see from your people" framing

The site never explicitly says "the people we describe here are composite / illustrative / based on real patterns we see in organizations like yours." That creates ambiguity for a sharp reader — are these real? Who authorized the publication? A single line of honesty — *"These are composite scenarios drawn from recurring patterns we see when we sit with expert practitioners. They're illustrative, not literal."* — builds trust and removes a distraction.

## Prioritized changes

If you only have time for a few things, do them in this order:

**1. Fix the metrics across all three cases (half a day).** Switch from efficiency metrics to transformation metrics. Cut "Days Build time" from all three heroes. This single change reframes the entire section in service of the POV.

**2. Rewrite Materials R&D narrative (half a day).** It's the weakest, the fix is clear, and it's the first case study most visitors will see (Consumer Goods tab first). The rewrite sketch above is a starting point.

**3. Add one human moment per case (two hours).** One line each. You'll feel the cases warm up considerably.

**4. Feature the "refused move" in each case (two hours).** Pull it out as a sidebar, a quote, or a bolded line. This is where the POV's thesis becomes visible.

**5. Vary the structural rhythm across the three (one day).** Different number of beats, different opening treatment, different dominant visual per case. This is the biggest lift but the biggest payoff for making them feel distinct.

**6. Add a "what happened next" postscript to each case (two hours).** Even one line. Closes the fluency loop for the reader.

**7. Small copy polish and disclaimer (one hour).** The illustrative-not-literal line, tighten the over-technical passages with translation voice, adjust the 1/5 navigation.

Total: ~2.5 days of focused work gets you from 50–60% to launch-ready.

## One last note on the POV itself

Reading the POV and the case studies together: **the POV is substantially stronger than the case studies right now.** The POV lays out a clear thesis ("expertise is what AI requires, not what it replaces"), and the case studies don't yet consistently prove that thesis — they're still partly stuck in efficiency language.

That's fixable. The good news: every case has at least one line that already lands the POV's thesis perfectly. Absorb has "removed everything between the expertise and the science." CaseBuilder has "the judgment is hers — the prep is what's been taken off her plate." ConceptLab has "outsourced → owned."

Those lines are already there. The work is to build the rest of each case around them — to make those lines the case study's *center of gravity* rather than its closing grace note.
