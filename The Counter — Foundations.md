# The Counter: Foundations

*The philosophies that guide us and the problems we're here to solve.*

---

## Why This Document Exists

Before we design tools, before we map a process, before we build a single thing for a client — we need to be honest about what we're actually doing and why it matters.

The Counter isn't an innovation consultancy that happens to use AI. It's a response to something we can feel happening right now: the meaning of work is being rewritten, and most people are watching it happen to them instead of shaping what it becomes. We left careers we loved because we believe the window to help people get ahead of that shift — not behind it — is open right now. It won't be open forever.

This document lays out two things. First, the founding philosophies that guide how we show up, what we believe, and what we're building toward. Not for a pitch deck. For us. Second, the core problems we'll actually encounter when we sit down with real teams inside real organizations and try to help them see their work differently. These are the hurdles our toolkit has to be designed around — the specific, human, often uncomfortable tensions that make this work hard and make it matter.

Everything else — the 5i process, the mindsets, the behaviors, the tools — flows from here.

## PART ONE: OUR FOUNDING PHILOSOPHIES

### What We Actually Believe

We believe that the people closest to the work are the ones best positioned to reimagine it. Not consultants. Not IT departments. Not vendors. The person who has spent ten years mastering a domain, who feels the friction every Tuesday afternoon, who knows instinctively what should be faster, better, or possible — that person is the starting point for everything.

We believe the technology has caught up to that instinct. For the first time, the gap between "I wish I had a tool that does this" and "I built a tool that does this" has functionally collapsed. What used to require a development team, a six-month roadmap, and a budget request can now happen in an afternoon, guided by someone who understands the problem deeply enough to describe it clearly.

We believe that most organizations are investing in the wrong layer. They're teaching people to use AI. They're not teaching people to think with AI. Literacy — knowing what the tools can do — is table stakes. Fluency — seeing your own expertise as the raw material for building new ways of working — is what actually changes things. That distinction is the heart of everything we do.

We believe that change doesn't land through presentations. It lands through evidence. When someone watches their daily frustration become a working solution in a few hours, something shifts that no slide deck can produce. That shift — the moment someone stops seeing themselves as a user of tools and starts seeing themselves as someone who defines tools — is the real product of our work.

And we believe this matters urgently. Not because the technology is moving fast (it is), but because the longer people spend being trained on yesterday's capability, the harder it becomes to close the gap. The organizations that figure this out now will have a compounding advantage. The ones that don't will spend years trying to catch up. We'd rather help people get ahead.

### Our Compass

Every engagement we run, every tool we design, every conversation we have should orient toward these commitments. This isn't a manifesto for clients. It's a compass for us.

**Purpose: We build fluency that compounds.**

Our job is not to deliver tools, although we do. Our job is not to run workshops, although our work involves working closely with teams. Our job is to create the conditions where people's existing expertise meets new capability, and something permanent shifts in how they see what's possible. The tools are evidence. The fluency is the product. If someone walks away from a Counter engagement with a great prototype but no change in how they think about their work, we haven't done our job.

**Values: How we hold ourselves accountable.**

*Proximity over authority.* We sit with people, not above them. The best insights come from the person who lives with the problem, not from the consultant who observes it. Our value is in how quickly we can translate someone's expertise into something tangible — not in having the answers ourselves.

*Evidence over opinion.* We don't tell organizations what AI could do. We show them. Working demonstrations, real reactions, documented proof. When a leadership team reads our output, they're not looking at what we think — they're looking at what their own people built and how those people responded.

*Honesty over comfort.* Some of the problems we surface will be uncomfortable. Not every idea warrants a build. Not every team is ready. Not every organization is structured to absorb what we reveal. We'd rather name that clearly than package false optimism.

*Joy in the work.* This should be energizing. For the people we work with, sitting down at The Counter should feel like possibility opening up, not like another corporate initiative to endure. If we're not creating genuine excitement — the kind where people start saying "what if we also..." unprompted — something is wrong with how we're showing up.

*Better work, not just faster work.* The capability we build should make people's work lives meaningfully better, not just more efficient on paper. We're not here to help companies squeeze more out of their people. We're here to help people reclaim the parts of their work that matter — the thinking, the expertise, the creativity — by removing the parts that don't.

**What We Invite People Into.**

When someone sits down at The Counter, they should experience something specific: We welcome what you know — your weird, deep, specific expertise that no one else has. We embrace the discomfort of building something new, because growth happens in the stretch, not the comfort zone. We encourage you to drive — this is your problem, your insight, your tool. We're here to accelerate, not to take the wheel. And we believe the thing you build here can create lasting, positive change — in your workflow, your team, maybe your entire organization.

That experience is the through-line from our first conversation with a buyer to the moment their people take a finished tool back to their desk. Every touchpoint should reinforce it.

## PART TWO: THE PROBLEMS WE'RE DESIGNING FOR

### A Different Game of Hurdles

We come from innovation consulting. We know the traditional hurdles: organizations that say they want new ideas but punish risk. Teams that brainstorm brilliantly but can't get past the pilot stage. Leaders who fund exploration but pull resources at the first sign of ambiguity. The classic innovation blockers — organizational inertia, fear of failure, political misalignment, the gap between strategy and execution — are real and they're not going away.

But what we're walking into with The Counter is a fundamentally different game. We're not asking people to come up with new product ideas or reimagine their customer experience. We're asking them to reconsider the basic mechanics of how they do their job. That's a deeper intervention, and it triggers a different set of responses.

The process we use — discovery, immersion, ideation, incubation, impact — is structurally familiar. It still works. But the problems sitting inside each phase are different when the subject is "how will you work in five years?" instead of "what new thing should we build for customers?" The hurdles below are what our toolkit needs to be specifically designed to address.

### Problem 1: The Identity Threat

**What it is:** For many people, their professional identity is inseparable from the skills they've spent years developing. When AI can do — or appear to do — significant portions of what they're known for, it doesn't just feel like a workflow change. It feels like an existential question. "If the thing I'm good at can be automated, what am I actually good at? What's my value?"

**Why it's different from traditional innovation resistance:** In a typical innovation engagement, you're asking people to apply their expertise in new ways. The expertise itself isn't threatened — it's the foundation. In an AI fluency engagement, the expertise is still the foundation, but people don't always see it that way. They see the technology doing something that took them years to learn, and they feel replaced before they feel empowered.

**What this means for our toolkit:** We need tools and approaches that make someone's expertise the explicit hero of the narrative, long before we ever show them what AI can do. The first thing someone should feel at The Counter isn't "AI is amazing" — it's "my knowledge is the thing that makes this possible." Every build should start from the person's domain mastery and show them that the technology amplifies their expertise rather than substituting for it. The moment someone sees their categories, their language, their judgment embedded in a working tool, the identity threat starts to dissolve. We need to engineer that moment deliberately.

### Problem 2: The Excitement-Terror Spectrum

**What it is:** In any given room of twenty people, you'll have a few who are thrilled by what AI can do, a few who are deeply unsettled by it, and a large middle who are some confused blend of both. This isn't a knowledge gap — it's an emotional spectrum. The excited people have already been experimenting on their own. The unsettled people have been reading headlines about job displacement and feeling a low hum of dread. The middle is waiting to see which narrative wins.

**Why it's different:** Traditional innovation work mostly deals with one emotional register: skepticism about whether a new idea will work. AI fluency work deals with a much wider and more volatile range. You can have someone go from excited to terrified in the span of a single demonstration, because the same capability that solves their problem also implies their problem-solving skills might become less valuable. Managing this spectrum isn't a facilitation nice-to-have — it's a core design requirement.

**What this means for our toolkit:** We need to understand where people sit on this spectrum before we engage, and we need tools that meet each position with honesty. For the excited, we need to channel that energy into structured building rather than letting it scatter into abstract enthusiasm. For the unsettled, we need approaches that give them control — starting from their terms, their pace, their problem — so the experience feels like agency rather than imposition. For the middle, we need the evidence to speak: real people, real problems, real tools built in real time. Our toolkit can't assume a uniform emotional starting point. It has to be designed for a room where someone is leaning forward and someone else is quietly panicking, and both responses are completely reasonable.

### Problem 3: The Abstraction Trap

**What it is:** Most people's understanding of AI is abstract. They know it exists. They've probably used ChatGPT. They may have attended a workshop. But they cannot connect "AI" to "the specific thing I do on Wednesday that takes four hours and shouldn't." The distance between the general concept and the personal application is enormous, and no amount of case studies about other companies or other roles closes it.

**Why it's different:** Traditional innovation engagements often deal in abstraction on purpose — you want people thinking big, imagining futures, exploring possibilities. The abstraction is generative. In AI fluency work, abstraction is the enemy. Every minute someone spends thinking about AI in general terms is a minute they're not spending on the only question that matters: what does this mean for my specific work? The abstraction trap keeps people intellectually engaged but practically stuck.

**What this means for our toolkit:** Everything has to be ruthlessly specific, ruthlessly fast, and ruthlessly personal. We need tools that collapse the distance between "AI can do things" and "AI just did this thing for you, with your data, in your language, solving your problem." The Problem Brief, the discovery conversation, the build sprint — every element of the process should be engineered to get to specificity as quickly as possible. The moment of impact isn't when someone understands what AI can do. It's when they see what it just did with their Tuesday afternoon.

### Problem 4: The Permission Problem

**What it is:** Even when people can see what's possible, many don't feel they have permission to act on it. Permission to spend time experimenting. Permission to build something outside the sanctioned tool stack. Permission to suggest that the current way of doing things is broken. Permission to be bad at something new. Organizations say they want innovation, but the daily signals — what gets rewarded, what gets questioned, what gets funded — often say otherwise. In the context of AI, this is compounded: people aren't just worried about pursuing a new idea, they're worried about touching a technology that their organization hasn't fully figured out its own stance on.

**Why it's different:** Traditional innovation programs often have explicit executive sponsorship and a defined "safe space" for experimentation. AI fluency work operates in a more ambiguous zone. The organization may have an AI strategy, but it probably lives in IT or digital transformation, and the person on the ground doesn't know how their individual experimentation relates to it. They're not sure if building a personal tool is encouraged or is shadow IT. They're not sure if changing their workflow is innovative or insubordinate.

**What this means for our toolkit:** We need to design explicit permission structures into the engagement. The buyer's involvement in team selection, the Problem Brief that teaches people what kind of problems to bring, the visible leadership support during the on-site — all of these are permission signals. But we also need tools that help people give themselves permission. Small experiments, low stakes, fast feedback loops. The experience of building something in two hours that would have taken six months to request through normal channels is itself a permission-granting event. Our toolkit should create a cascading series of these moments: each small success makes the next experiment feel less risky.

### Problem 5: The Capability Cliff

**What it is:** There's a real gap between watching someone build an AI-powered tool and being able to do it yourself. The Counter experience is designed to create a powerful demonstration of what's possible — but if people leave without a pathway to independent capability, the energy dissipates. This is the "what happens Monday" problem, and it's the single biggest risk to our model.

**Why it's different:** In traditional innovation, the deliverable is often an idea, a strategy, or a prototype that gets handed off to another team to implement. The capability to execute doesn't need to live in the same people who generated the insight. In AI fluency work, the entire point is that the capability lives in the person. If Maria the dispute analyst can't continue to explore and build after we leave, we haven't actually shifted her capability — we've just given her a nice demo and a temporary high.

**What this means for our toolkit:** We need a deliberate capability-building arc within every engagement, not just a "reveal" moment. The progression matters: first, someone sees what's possible (watching us build). Then, they participate in the building (working alongside us). Then, they modify what was built (extending it on their own with our support). Then, they build something new (applying the thinking to a different problem). Our tools need to support each stage of this arc, and our engagement structure needs to allocate time for the later stages, not just the impressive early ones. The Counter Log, the resources we leave behind, the way we design the tools themselves to be transparent and modifiable — all of this serves the capability arc. If we're not designing for independence, we're just a fancier version of the consulting model we're trying to replace.

### Problem 6: The Organizational Immune Response

**What it is:** Organizations have antibodies against change. Not malicious ones — structural ones. Procurement processes that take months. IT security reviews that gate any new tool. Middle managers whose incentives are built around the current workflow. Budget cycles that don't have a line item for "things we didn't know we needed until someone built them in two hours." When someone comes back from The Counter excited and carrying a working tool, the organization's existing systems will naturally resist integrating it — not because anyone is against progress, but because the systems weren't designed for this kind of speed.

**Why it's different:** Traditional innovation consulting typically operates at the strategic level — the output is a recommendation that enters the organization's existing decision-making process. The Counter's output is working tools and activated people who want to use them immediately. That's a different entry point, and it collides with organizational systems in different ways. The enthusiasm is more personal, the proof is more tangible, and the frustration when it gets stuck in a review process is more acute.

**What this means for our toolkit:** We need tools that help people navigate their own organizations after we leave. This isn't just about building good prototypes — it's about equipping people with the language, the evidence, and the frameworks to make the case internally. The Leadership Portfolio and Counter Log serve this purpose at the strategic level, but we also need to think about the individual level: how does Maria take her dispute analysis tool and get it sanctioned? How does she describe what it does to IT? How does she quantify the value for her manager? Our toolkit should include lightweight advocacy tools — not because our people should have to fight for progress, but because they will, and we should arm them for it.

### Problem 7: The Uneven Starting Line

**What it is:** People come to this with wildly different baselines. Some have been experimenting with AI on their own for months and are already frustrated that their organization is slow. Others have never used anything beyond basic office tools and find the entire concept disorienting. Some are technical by training; others are domain experts who've never written a formula more complex than an Excel SUM. Age, role, background, comfort with technology, relationship with change itself — all of these create an uneven playing field.

**Why it's different:** Traditional innovation engagements can use a shared process as the equalizer — everyone follows the same methodology, and the playing field levels through the structure. AI fluency work hits a more fundamental asymmetry: some people walk in already believing they can build things, and others walk in believing this is something that happens to them, not something they do. That's not a knowledge gap — it's a self-concept gap. And it requires different approaches for different people within the same engagement.

**What this means for our toolkit:** We need modular entry points, not a single on-ramp. The way we engage the person who's already been vibe coding on weekends should look different from how we engage the person who's been quietly anxious about whether they're going to be replaced. Both need to feel the same core thing — that their expertise is the starting point — but the path to that feeling runs through different territory. Our tools should allow for variable pacing, variable depth, and variable starting assumptions, all within the same engagement structure. This is where the innovation foundations — meeting people where they are, signaling what mindset we're in, creating psychological safety — become essential. Not as nice-to-have facilitation techniques, but as structural requirements for the work landing at all.

### Problem 8: The Meaning Question

**What it is:** Underneath all the practical concerns — will this work, can I do it, will my organization support it — there's a deeper question that most people won't say out loud: "If AI can do all of this, what is my work actually for?" This isn't resistance. It's philosophy. And it's the question that will increasingly define how people relate to their professional lives. The organizations and the individuals who develop a genuine, honest answer to this question will thrive. The ones who paper over it with productivity metrics will struggle.

**Why it's different:** Traditional innovation consulting doesn't usually touch this layer. You're helping people create new value, not question the nature of their existing value. AI fluency work can't avoid it. The moment someone sees their eight-hour report generated in seconds, the question is in the room whether you name it or not. If we don't engage it — thoughtfully, honestly, without false reassurance — it becomes the invisible undertow that pulls against everything we build.

**What this means for our toolkit:** We need at least one tool or framework that helps people sit with this question productively. Not to answer it — nobody has the answer yet, and pretending otherwise is dishonest. But to reframe it. The Counter's implicit answer is: your value isn't in the output you produce; it's in the judgment, creativity, and domain knowledge that determines what gets built and why. Maria's value isn't the weekly dispute report. It's the ten years of pattern recognition that makes the tool's output meaningful. James's value isn't the guest feedback synthesis. It's the ability to know which patterns matter and what to do about them. Our toolkit should make this reframe explicit — not as a platitude, but as something people experience firsthand when they watch their expertise become the engine of something new.

## What Comes Next

This document is the foundation. What follows is the harder, more detailed work: adapting the 5i process for AI fluency engagements, defining the mindsets and behaviors that matter most in this specific context, and designing the individual tools that address the problems laid out above.

But every tool we build, every exercise we design, every facilitation choice we make should be traceable back to something in this document. If a tool doesn't serve one of these philosophies or address one of these problems, it doesn't belong in our toolkit.

The innovation methodology we come from taught us that making change easy and approachable is the single most important design principle. That hasn't changed. What's changed is the nature of the change we're asking people to make — and the stakes of getting it right.
