How The Counter Works

PHASE 01 · ~1 WEEK

We partner with you to find the right starting point.

Before we arrive, we help you identify the people and problems that are the best fit for this experience. Specifically, those who have something they're working on right now that they wish was faster, better, or simply possible. It could be something for their team, their customers, or just themselves.

What happens in the room

Structured interviews and observation sessions with 4–8 team leads. We use our Spectrum Map diagnostic to plot where each person sits on the literacy-to-fluency continuum — not to judge readiness, but to identify where the highest-leverage starting points live.

What you get

A one-page engagement brief with the specific workflow we'll target, the team we'll work with, and the hypothesis we're testing.

PHASE 02 · 2 WEEKS

We build working solutions with your teams in a few hours.

In pre-scheduled sessions, individuals sit down with us and their problem. Working side by side, we help them develop AI fluency in real time — learning how to think with AI to diagnose their problem, explore a wide range of potential solutions, and build a minimum viable product.

What happens in the room

Facilitated build sprints using our Rope of Possibility method — we start from what they already know about their work and stretch toward what AI makes newly possible. Teams use the Tuesday Test: if what they build wouldn't change how they work next Tuesday, we rebuild it.

What you get

Working AI-augmented workflows built by your people, tested on real tasks, with documented methodology so they can iterate independently.

PHASE 03 · 3 WEEKS

They test what they built, and we iterate together.

Individuals take the solution they built and start using and testing it. A few weeks later, we reconnect to hear what's working, what's not, and iterate on their solution together to improve it.

What happens in the room

Weekly iteration sessions. Teams bring what worked and what didn't. We use the Expertise Flip — examining moments where AI's output was wrong or shallow, and showing how the person's domain expertise was the thing that caught it, refined it, made it actually useful. This reframes the relationship: AI isn't replacing their judgment, it's requiring it.

What you get

Refined workflows that have survived real use. Teams who can articulate why AI helps and where it doesn't — which matters more than the tools themselves.

PHASE 04 · LONG AFTER WE LEAVE

They become proof for the whole org.

Multiple people across your organization have built real solutions and developed the fluency to keep going on their own. And you have the proof and real outcomes to take this further, to more teams, and at a bigger scale across your organization.

What happens in the room

We equip your initial teams with the Monday Map — a framework for showing others what changed and why. Not a presentation template; a way of making their transformation legible to peers who weren't in the room.

What you get

Internal advocates who can articulate and demonstrate AI fluency to their peers. The beginning of organic spread — transformation that scales through proof, not mandate.

01 — Ten Years, Live

The data she'd written but not read is now answering her back.

Every historical test cycle — nearly 600 results across ten years of polymer work — is queryable in context. She used to fly past the 2017 dataset on her way to this week's results. Now she can open any moment of her own history with a single question.

02 — The Hypothesis She'd Held Onto

Particle size vs. absorption speed. The non-linear interaction she'd suspected for years, now on screen.

She'd had an instinct about this — something about surfactant level interacting with particle size that wasn't linear. She'd never had time to prove it. Instead of running comparisons one variable at a time, she can now see every predictive relationship at once. The answer arrives in seconds.

“The first thing I noticed wasn't the speed. It was that the questions were starting to come from me again — not from the backlog.”

03 — The Next Twenty Questions

With each answer, the next question appears.

Dial in a combination of parameters — particle size, surfactant level, viscosity — and the tool surfaces the closest historical matches with predicted performance ranges. She stops asking what she has time for and starts asking what she's curious about.

The Tool's Limit
The tool doesn't propose the experiments. It needs Sarah to dial in the parameters — because her instinct about which variables matter is the experiment.

04 — The Report Becomes a Byproduct

The afternoon that used to disappear is now a side effect.

Committee-ready documentation — cycle performance against baseline, anomalies caught, ranking summaries — assembled from the analysis she's already done. Writing the report used to consume her Thursday. It is now generated from the thinking, not in addition to it.

The Shift

The tool didn't make Sarah faster at her existing work. It gave her back the work she'd stopped trying to do.

She wasn't slow before — she was buried. A decade of results sat in files she never opened on her way to the next cycle. The tool removed everything between her expertise and her science.

What Happened Next
Two weeks later, Sarah had run seventeen more hypotheses and queued three for the next cycle review — including the one she'd written down in 2019 and almost forgotten.

01 — The Brief

The strategic question, her patient data, her approved claims — in one place.

Before she could get to concepts, she needed the foundation assembled. The brief, the segments, the raw patient insights, the claims her legal team had already cleared. The tool starts here — with her material, not generic pharma prompts.

02 — Concept Foundations

Four strategic directions, not four slogans.

Each concept is a full strategic foundation — target segment, patient insight provenance, tagline, headline, and body copy. Built to hand to her agency for visual production, drop into MLR in parallel, or test directly in qualitative research.

03 — Traceable to Source

Every concept cites the patient insight that shaped it.

No vague ‘patient-centric’ claims. Each direction carries the IDs of the specific insights behind it — so when a VP asks ‘where did that come from?’, the answer is one click away.

"When the first four concepts appeared, I realized something. The agency had been doing this work — but not this way. The strategy had never been this legible."

The Tool's Limit
The tool doesn't invent the concepts. It surfaces the ones her own data was already telling her.

04 — Patient + HCP in Parallel

HCP variants used to mean a second brief. Now they ship together.

Same strategic foundation, tailored language for each audience. She can toggle between the patient and rheumatologist version of any concept — and both are ready to leave her desk the same afternoon.

The Shift

Same brand lead. Same data. A fundamentally different process.

The strategic foundation work used to be outsourced — to agencies, to creative directors, to people who didn't know her data. It's hers now. The compressed timeline is the visible part. The role change is the structural part.

What Happened Next
Rachel took the eight concepts into MLR review the same week. Two cleared on first pass. The agency's role shifted from strategic intermediary to visual execution partner by the following cycle.

01 — Read the Case, Not Its Pieces

Narratives, rebuttal, and transaction record in one view.

She used to open three systems to assemble a single case: the cardholder's narrative, the merchant's rebuttal, and the raw transaction record. The tool parses all three into one view so the full fact pattern is available in one read.

02 — Surface the Contradictions

The cross-examination she ran in her head, now on the page.

Every case has tells: internal inconsistency, temporal mistakes in the merchant's dates, claims the narrative conspicuously avoids. The tool pulls them out and categorizes them — so she spots the moves a newer analyst might miss.

03 — Separate Claims from Evidence

A chip-read and a camera claim are not the same.

Every strength and weakness carries a tag — VERIFIED when an artifact or system record backs it, ASSERTED when it's only been claimed. A merchant's unproduced camera footage no longer sits in the same column as the chip-read that actually happened.

04 — Scaffold, Don't Summarize

Facts established. Facts in dispute. Open questions. Then it stops.

The tool assembles everything needed for the determination and puts the blank determination box next to it.

The Tool's Limit
It does not propose a verdict, on purpose. The judgment is hers — the prep is what's been taken off her plate.

The Shift

Same analyst. Same queue. Completely different afternoon.

The tool didn't make Maya faster at adjudicating. She was always fast. It stopped making her rebuild the case file twenty times a day.

"The dread used to start on Mondays. Three cases in the queue before I sat down. Now I get to actually think about each one."

What Happened Next
Three weeks in, Maya was pulling aside junior analysts to show them the contradiction patterns she used to run in her head. The tool hadn't replaced her judgment. It had made it teachable.
